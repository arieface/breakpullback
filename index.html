Market Structure & Break of Structure (BOS)

Market structure is the framework all professional price action trading is built on. In any trending market, price advances in higher highs and higher lows (uptrend) or lower highs and lower lows (downtrend). These swing points are structural levels — the price zones where institutional participants concentrate orders.

Break of Structure (BOS) occurs when price breaks above a significant swing high in an uptrend, or below a swing low in a downtrend. This is not just any breakout — it is structural confirmation that institutional players have committed capital to a direction. In SMC and ICT methodology, BOS is the primary signal for trend continuation entries.

Change of Character (CHOCH) is the counter — when price breaks structure in the opposite direction to the prevailing trend, signalling a potential reversal. Understanding the difference between BOS and CHOCH is the foundation of reading market intent.

The Pullback & Retest Entry

The single principle that separates profitable breakout traders from the rest: never enter on the initial break — wait for the pullback.

When price breaks a structural level, that level flips function. A swing high that was resistance becomes support once broken. Price typically returns to retest this flipped level before continuing. This pullback is the high-probability entry window — better price, tighter stop, and confirmation that the flip held.

The Break → Pullback → Continuation sequence is the structure of every institutional trend-following trade. It works on Forex, Gold, and all liquid markets across M15 to D1.

HTF Daily Bias & Three Candle Monitor

HTF bias is the second critical filter most retail traders ignore entirely. A breakout on H1 that aligns with the Daily trend has fundamentally higher probability than one taken against it.

The Daily timeframe represents the institutional view. Banks and funds build positions from daily and weekly charts. Taking a bearish H1 breakout against a bullish Daily structure means standing opposite the largest market participants.

The Three Candle Monitor provides an unambiguous bias reading: examine the last three daily candles. Higher closes indicate bullish bias; lower closes indicate bearish. This filter typically eliminates 30–40% of trades — but the remaining entries carry meaningfully higher win rates.

Currency Strength Index (CSI) & Rate of Change

A question most traders never ask: when EURUSD breaks upward, is EUR strengthening — or is USD simply weakening? The pair chart cannot tell you. Only by measuring each currency individually can you determine which side is driving the move.

Currency Strength Index (CSI) measures the individual momentum of each currency using Rate of Change (ROC) — comparing its current price against a basket to its price N periods prior. A positive ROC signals genuine strengthening; negative signals weakness.

If CSI shows GBP is the strongest currency while USD is the weakest during a GBPUSD breakout, that is a high-conviction institutional move. If GBP is actually weak, the breakout lacks real momentum — you avoid it and preserve capital.
